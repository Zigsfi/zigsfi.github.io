I started to code at age 11 with basic forays into web development; I learned the basics of HTML from my father and put together some basic websites locally using OpenOffice. At age 12, I took my first step into imperative programming with Dark Basic Classic, then called Dark Basic. The simple, readable syntax was enough to set me off developing games, a hobby that I have continued to this day. By seventh grade, I had intuited the standard "game loop" design framework, and used it to develop several fledgling projects in the newer Dark Basic Pro. I rarely delved deeply into projects, preferring to experiment with new ideas instead of developing old ones.

As I entered High School, I began to develop networked multiplayer games to play with my friends on the school's LAN.

Sophomore year, I began taking classes in Computer Programming, and was introduced to my first Object-Oriented Language, Java.

As a senior in high school, I embarked on a guided study on iOS development.

First semester of freshman year, I took Data Structures, and learned C++ in the process. The next semester, I took Machine Structure and Assembly Language Programming, a class for which I, over the course of two semesters, was a teaching assistant. I also took Discrete Mathematics.

As a first-semester sophomore, I took Internet Scale Distributed Systems, a special topics course taught by the IETF's own Noah Mendelsohn. I also took Algorithms. I also participated in my first Hackathon, in which my project, Echoes won a prize for best use of the EchoNest api. In the second semester of sophomore year, I took Programming Languages, for which I am currently a teaching assistant. This class introduced me to functional programming, a topic which fascinates me to this day. I also took Game Development.
